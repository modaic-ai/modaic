Modaic uses frontier research in mechanistic interpretability to measure confidence in LLM judge decisions. Unlike traditional approaches, our confidence estimators improve continuously as you label more data — they learn the specific failure modes of your judge on your tasks.

## How Transformer Models Work

Most LLMs today use the *Transformer architecture*. Input text is split into tokens, each mapped to a learned embedding vector. These vectors pass through a series of layers — each containing a self-attention mechanism, a feed-forward network, and layer normalization — that progressively build up rich contextual representations.

The output of the final layer for the last generated token is a dense vector called the **hidden state** (or residual stream). This vector encodes the model's full understanding of the context and is projected through a linear output head to produce a probability distribution over the next token.

## Existing Approaches to Confidence Estimation

Several methods exist for estimating uncertainty in LLMs, each with significant limitations.

**Verbalized Confidence**

The model is prompted to output a numerical confidence score alongside its answer. This is the least reliable approach: LLMs are systematically overconfident and will express high certainty even when they are wrong.

**Token Log-Probabilities**

Confidence is derived from the probability scores assigned to individual output tokens. Two problems make this unreliable: (1) a response spans many tokens, and aggregating per-token probabilities into a single calibrated score is non-trivial; (2) raw log-probabilities are not well-calibrated to real-world accuracy.

**Self-Consistency / Ensemble Disagreement**

The same model is sampled multiple times (self-consistency), or a panel of different models each produce an answer (ensemble), and disagreement between outputs is used as an uncertainty signal. Calibration is better than verbalized confidence, but this approach is expensive and degrades significantly for tasks that were underrepresented in the models' training data.

## How Modaic Is Different

Modaic reads the **hidden activations** — the latent vector produced just before the output token distribution is computed — and uses a learned probe to predict whether the judge's decision is correct.

This probe is a small model trained to recognize patterns in the activation space that correlate with errors. When you click *Align*, the probe is fine-tuned on your newly labeled examples, so it continuously adapts to the specific failure modes of your judge on your evaluation task.

Because the probe operates on internal model state rather than just the output, it can capture signals that output-only methods miss:

- How well the input sample falls within the scope of the system prompt
- How similar the sample is to past examples where the judge has failed
- Whether the model's internal reasoning is consistent with the label it produced

This is why Modaic's confidence estimators achieve state-of-the-art performance on both AUROC and ECE for LLM judge tasks.
